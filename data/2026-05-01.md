*Zi-Xu Lu,Gang Liu,Matteo Fadel,Jie Li*

Main category: quant-ph

TL;DR: This paper proposes the first protocol for preparing magnonic Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) states using an ellipsoidal magnetic crystal coupled to a superconducting qubit via a microwave cavity, enabling bosonic quantum error correction in magnonic systems.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Bosonic quantum error correction with GKP states is powerful for protecting qubits against displacement errors, but GKP states have not yet been realized in magnonic platforms. The authors aim to extend GKP encoding to magnon systems, leveraging their unique properties.

Method: The protocol uses an ellipsoidal magnetic crystal whose geometric anisotropy provides intrinsic squeezing of the magnon mode. A superconducting qubit is coupled to the magnon mode via a microwave cavity. By applying two rounds of conditional-displacement interaction and qubit projective measurement, three- and four-component magnonic GKP-like states are generated.

Result: The protocol successfully produces three- and four-component magnonic GKP-like states. Additionally, single logical qubit gate operations (Pauli, Hadamard, and phase gates) are realized, completing the logical Pauli basis of the approximate GKP code.

Conclusion: Hybrid magnon-qubit systems represent a promising platform for preparing bosonic code states, with potential applications in magnonic fault-tolerant quantum computation and quantum sensing.

Abstract: Bosonic quantum error correction encodes a logical qubit in an oscillator, avoiding the hardware overhead of large qubit arrays. Among such encodings, Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) states are paticularly powerful because their phase-space grid structure protects against small displacement errors simultaneously in both conjugate quadratures. Here we provide the first protocol for preparing magnonic GKP states, which involves an ellipsoidal magnetic crystal effectively coupled to a superconducting qubit via a microwave cavity. The geometric anisotropy intrinsically squeezes the magnon mode, while the cavity-mediated qubit control realizes an effective conditional-displacement interaction. We show that two rounds of a conditional-displacement interaction and a qubit projective measurement yield three- and four-component magnonic GKP-like states. We also show how to realize single logical qubit gate operations, such as Pauli, Hadamard and phase gates, completing the logical Pauli basis of the approximate GKP code. Our results establish hybrid magnon-qubit systems as a promising platform for preparing bosonic code states, with applications in magnonic fault-tolerant quantum computation and quantum sensing.

### [58] [Quantum Lattice Boltzmann Solutions for Transport under 3D Spatially Varying Advection on Trapped Ion Hardware](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.28121)
*Sayonee Ray,Jezer Jojo,Jason Iaconis,Abeynaya Gnanasekaran,Apurva Tiwari,Martin Roetteler,Chris Hill,Jay Pathak*

Main category: quant-ph

TL;DR: This paper advances the Quantum Lattice Boltzmann Method (QLBM) for solving advection-diffusion equations on quantum hardware, demonstrating non-uniform velocity fields on IonQ trapped-ion systems, identifying density readout bottlenecks, and proposing MPS shadow tomography and novel wall boundary methods to improve scalability.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to advance QLBM from idealized settings towards more realistic CFD requirements, specifically addressing transport under non-uniform velocity fields on actual quantum hardware—a step toward practical quantum simulation of fluid dynamics.

Method: The authors implement QLBM on IonQ trapped-ion quantum systems (Forte and a 64-qubit Barium development system), using the advection-diffusion equation as a model problem. They analyze density readout bottlenecks, propose MPS shadow tomography for efficient readout scaling, and introduce a novel method for implementing wall boundaries.

Result: First demonstration of non-uniform velocity field transport in QLBM on quantum hardware. Identified density readout/reloading as a key bottleneck. MPS shadow tomography shows promise for scaling. Novel wall boundary method introduced and simulated.

Conclusion: QLBM is progressing toward realistic CFD problems, but density readout remains a critical bottleneck. MPS shadow tomography and new wall boundary implementations offer pathways to scale to higher-complexity problems.

Abstract: The Quantum Lattice Boltzmann Method (QLBM) has emerged as one of the most promising quantum computing approaches for the numerical simulation of problems in computational fluid dynamics (CFD). The dynamics is formulated in terms of mesoscopic particle distribution functions governed by a discrete Boltzmann transport equation, comprising local streaming and collision operations. In this work, the resulting macroscopic behavior corresponds to the advection-diffusion equation, which we adopt as a canonical model problem for transport phenomena. Building upon recent progress in QLBM implementations, we advance towards more realistic problem settings that better reflect conventional CFD requirements. We address, for the first time, transport under the action of non uniform velocity fields on quantum hardware. We implement our demonstration using IonQ's trapped-ion systems including Forte generation systems and a 64-qubit Barium development system similar to the forthcoming IonQ Tempo line. We identify the density readout and subsequent reloading of the fluid density as a potential bottleneck of the current algorithm and discuss several approaches to mitigate this bottleneck. We identify the use of MPS shadow tomography as a promising method to efficiently scale the readout to large system with complex density distributions. Lastly, we introduce and simulate a novel method to implement wall boundaries for advection-diffusion in QLBM, and discuss the prospects of scaling to higher-complexity problems.

</details>

### [59] [A Short Note on Batch-efficient Divide-and-Conquer Algorithm for EigenDecomposition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27325)
*Yue Song*

Main category: cs.LG

TL;DR: This paper proposes a batch-efficient Divide-and-Conquer EigenDecomposition algorithm for larger matrices (dim < 64) that is faster than PyTorch SVD, improving upon a prior QR-based method limited to small matrices (dim < 32).


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: EigenDecomposition is computationally expensive, especially for mini-batches of matrices in deep neural networks, creating a bottleneck. Prior work only addressed very small matrices (dim < 32).

Method: Proposes a batch-efficient Divide-and-Conquer based ED algorithm for matrices with dimensions smaller than 64.

Result: For mini-batch matrices with dimensions smaller than 64, the proposed method is much faster than the PyTorch SVD function.

Conclusion: The Divide-and-Conquer based ED algorithm effectively overcomes the dimensionality limitation of the prior QR-based approach, enabling faster computation for larger matrices in batch settings.

Abstract: EigenDecomposition (ED) is at the heart of many computer vision algorithms and applications. One crucial bottleneck limiting its usage is the expensive computation cost, particularly for a mini-batch of matrices in deep neural networks. Our previous work proposed a dedicated QR-based ED algorithm for batched small matrices (dim${<}32$). This short paper targets the limitation and proposes a batch-efficient Divide-and-Conquer based ED algorithm for larger matrices. The numerical test shows that for a mini-batch of matrices whose dimensions are smaller than $64$, our method can be much faster than the Pytorch SVD function.

</details>
