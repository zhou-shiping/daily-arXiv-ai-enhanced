### [56] [Randomized Sketching is Robust to Low-Precision Rounding on GPUs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20195)
*Aryaman Jeendgar,Clément Flint,Hartwig Anzt*

Main category: cs.PF

TL;DR: This paper studies mixed-precision GPU implementations of sparse subspace embeddings (SparseStack) and finds that FP16 SparseStack embedding quality is insensitive to the rounding rule, with deterministic rounding providing the best performance-accuracy tradeoff.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Sketching performance on GPUs is limited by memory traffic and atomic accumulation, not floating-point throughput, making it a natural target for mixed precision. The question is whether low-precision accumulation degrades embedding quality.

Method: The authors implement FP16 SparseStack variants using deterministic round-to-nearest, exact stochastic rounding, and dithered rounding, and compare with FP32 SparseStack, CountSketch, mixed-precision CountSketch, and FlashSketch.

Result: SparseStack embedding quality is insensitive to the FP16 rounding rule. The dominant accuracy factor is the sketch distribution rather than quantization rule. Deterministic rounding has the lowest overhead and best performance-accuracy tradeoff.

Conclusion: Mixed-precision FP16 SparseStack with deterministic rounding provides an effective performance-accuracy tradeoff, especially on coherent inputs where SparseStack variants outperform CountSketch.

Abstract: Randomized sketching is a core primitive in randomized numerical linear algebra. On modern hardware architectures, in particular on GPUs, the performance of sparse sketches is limited by memory traffic and atomic accumulation rather than floating-point throughput. This makes sketching a natural target for mixed precision, provided that low-precision accumulation does not degrade the embedding quality.
  We study mixed-precision GPU implementations of sparse oblivious subspace embeddings, focusing on a SparseStack generalization of the GPU CountSketch kernel of Higgins et al. SparseStack improves embedding quality relative to CountSketch on coherent inputs, but its additional nonzeros per column increase atomic-update contention and reduce throughput. We therefore implement FP16 SparseStack variants using deterministic round-to-nearest, exact stochastic rounding, and dithered rounding, and compare them with FP32 SparseStack, CountSketch, mixed-precision CountSketch, and FlashSketch.
  Our main empirical finding is that, for the tested regimes, SparseStack embedding quality is insensitive to the FP16 rounding rule. Deterministic, stochastic, and dithered rounding FP16 SparseStack produce nearly identical subspace distortion and sketch-and-solve least-squares accuracy across incoherent, coherent, and adversarial test problems. The dominant accuracy factor is the sketch distribution rather than the quantization rule: SparseStack variants substantially improve distortion on coherent inputs, while all methods behave similarly on incoherent inputs. Since deterministic rounding has the lowest overhead, it provides the best performance--accuracy tradeoff among the FP16 SparseStack variants.

### [57] [Acceleration methods for the planar 3D ILSA hydraulic fracturing model](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20025)
*V. I. Shukalo,A. V. Valov,A. N. Baykin*

Main category: physics.geo-ph

TL;DR: This paper proposes acceleration strategies for planar 3D hydraulic fracturing simulations (ILSA), including a unified solver, matrix splitting, Anderson acceleration, and predictor-corrector, achieving up to 11x speedup with less than 5% error.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Planar 3D models are accurate but too slow for large ensembles needed in treatment design optimization and mini-frac test interpretation; the goal is to reduce runtime while preserving accuracy.

Method: (1) A unified ILSA scheme consolidating nested loops into a single iterative process; (2) matrix splitting to yield a sparse system matrix; (3) Anderson acceleration for improved convergence; (4) a predictor-corrector scheme for combined assessment.

Result: Unified scheme: average 2.5x speedup (up to 5.7x). Combined techniques: average 4x speedup (up to 11x). Fracture aperture discrepancy below 5% vs. reference.

Conclusion: The proposed methods significantly accelerate planar 3D ILSA simulations while maintaining accuracy, enabling large-scale hydraulic fracturing studies.

Abstract: Planar 3D models of hydraulic fracturing provide a practical balance between models with restrictive geometric assumptions and fully 3D simulators, capturing fractures with arbitrary planar footprints at moderate computational cost. Nevertheless, applications such as treatment design optimization and mini-frac test interpretation require large ensembles of simulations, for which the cost of planar 3D models remains a significant bottleneck. This work presents acceleration strategies for the planar 3D Implicit Level Set Algorithm (ILSA) to reduce simulation runtime while preserving numerical accuracy. A unified planar 3D ILSA scheme that consolidates the nested loops of the elastohydrodynamic solver and the front tracking algorithm into a single iterative process is introduced. A matrix splitting approach is applied to the linearized elastohydrodynamic system, moving the dense part of the elasticity operator to the right-hand side and yielding a sparse system matrix that can be solved more efficiently. Anderson acceleration is incorporated into the solution of the elastohydrodynamic system to improve convergence under varying fracture geometry. Additionally, a predictor--corrector scheme is examined with the proposed methods to assess their combined effect. Each technique is evaluated individually and in combination on both the reference and unified planar 3D ILSA schemes across five benchmark cases. Numerical experiments demonstrate that the unified scheme alone delivers an average 2.5x speedup, reaching 5.7x for the sandglass geometry. The combined application of all techniques achieves an average 4x speedup and up to 11x for the sandglass case, with the relative discrepancy in fracture aperture below 5% compared with the reference scheme.

### [58] [Can DFT-trained neural network potentials reproduce structure, solvation, and water-exchange properties in aqueous magnesium solutions?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20105)
*Sebastian Falkner,Pablo Montero de Hijes,Christoph Dellago,Nadine Schwierz*

Main category: physics.chem-ph

TL;DR: This paper develops and benchmarks MACE neural network potentials for aqueous MgCl₂ solutions, showing they accurately reproduce hydration structure, diffusion, ion pairing, and water-exchange kinetics from DFT reference data, but significantly underestimate solvation free energy, highlighting the need for explicit long-range electrostatic treatments.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Magnesium ions are crucial in biological processes but classical force fields fail to simultaneously reproduce structural, thermodynamic, and kinetic solution properties because they cannot account for quantum many-body effects. The authors aim to overcome this limitation using DFT-trained neural network potentials.

Method: The authors developed MACE neural network potentials trained on revPBE-D3/zd and revPBE0-D3/zd DFT reference data, and assessed them against experimental solution properties including hydration shell structure, diffusion coefficients, activity derivatives, water-exchange rates/mechanisms, and solvation free energy via enhanced sampling techniques like transition path sampling and transition interface sampling.

Result: Both NNPs accurately reproduce octahedral hydration shell structure, ion pairing, and diffusion coefficients. Water-exchange kinetics (dissociative mechanism) were captured within one order of magnitude of experiment. However, the solvation free energy was significantly underestimated, revealing a limitation of local NNP architectures.

Conclusion: DFT-trained NNPs can accurately describe Mg²⁺ hydration structure, diffusion, ion pairing, and exchange kinetics, but explicit long-range electrostatic treatments are needed for quantitative agreement with experimental solvation free energies.

Abstract: Magnesium ions play an essential role in many biological processes but remain challenging to model in biomolecular simulations. Despite considerable scientific effort, classical force fields fail to simultaneously reproduce key structural, thermodynamic and kinetic solution properties, likely due to their inability to explicitly account for quantum many-body effects. Here, we develop and systematically benchmark MACE neural network potentials (NNPs) for aqueous MgCl$_2$ solutions trained on revPBE-D3/zd and revPBE0-D3/zd density functional theory reference data and assess their ability to reproduce a broad range of experimental solution properties including the structure of the first hydration shell, diffusion coefficient, activity derivative, water-exchange rate and mechanism as well as solvation free energy. Both NNPs accurately reproduce the octahedral structure of the first hydration shell, ion pairing properties and diffusion coefficients. Combining the NNPs with transition path sampling and other enhanced sampling techniques allows us to capture the rare event of water exchange in the first hydration shell of Mg$^{2+}$ revealing a dissociative exchange mechanism. Transition interface sampling yields exchange rates within one order of magnitude of experiment, representing a substantial improvement over classical dissociative force fields. In contrast, the NNP-derived solvation free energy significantly underestimates the experimental value, revealing a limitation of the present local NNP architectures for describing ion solvation thermodynamics. Our results demonstrate that DFT-trained NNPs can accurately describe Mg$^{2+}$ hydration structure, diffusion, ion pairing, and exchange kinetics, while highlighting the need for explicit long-range electrostatic treatments to achieve quantitative agreement with experimental ion solvation free energies.

</details>
